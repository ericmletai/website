When the Price Tag Became a Moving Target

Published by Eric Le Tai

How Digital Labels Are Quietly Eroding Consumer Trust

At the end of the day it comes down to one thing — trust. Right now, that trust is being quietly spent.

At its core, a digital price tag is just a tool. Like any technology, an electronic shelf label doesn't arrive with an agenda — it's a screen, a signal, and a piece of software. From a purely technical standpoint, it's neither predatory nor generous. It simply does what it's told.

But history has a way of filling in that blank. Consumers have watched the same story unfold across industries — streaming services that raised rates once the market was locked in, ride-share apps that invented surge pricing and called it demand-based fairness, social platforms that monetized attention and framed it as connection. New technology enters as a neutral promise and exits as a revenue mechanism.

"Whether or not businesses actually use ESLs to benefit end consumers as opposed to their own bottom line, however, still largely remains to be seen."

— Mack DeGeurin, Popular Science

The larger the company, the quieter the explanation of who actually benefits most. Shoppers are rarely told the full picture upfront — they're expected to figure it out themselves, often after the fact. That means the responsibility, as it so often does, lands on the consumer. Staying informed and staying skeptical isn't paranoia — it's the baseline now. Here's where to start:

Three Things Every Shopper Should Do Now

  • Check back — the price may change. Treat grocery pricing the way you'd treat booking a flight. A little patience or a return trip could mean real savings.

  • Watch for surge pricing on holidays and peak hours. High-demand moments are exactly when these systems are designed to capitalize. Plan ahead and shop early when you can.

  • Triple-check labels and read the fine print. Verify prices at the shelf, at the register, and on your receipt. The era of taking a price at face value is over.

None of this should feel like extra work — but it does, and that's the point.

Shoppers who feel the squeeze will learn to wait out better prices, comparison shop more aggressively, and treat big-box stores as a last resort. Some may abandon the ecosystem entirely — gravitating toward local farmers markets, co-ops, and independent grocers where the price on the sign is simply the price.

How far that shift goes depends on how aggressively corporations choose to use this tool and how honest they're willing to be about it. The gaslighting will come packaged as consumer benefit — efficiency savings, loyalty discounts, markdowns on expiring goods. Some of it may even be true. But the erosion of trust compounds quietly. Every unexplained price jump, every receipt that doesn't match the shelf — it all adds up. And once a shopper stops believing the price tag, they stop believing the store.
